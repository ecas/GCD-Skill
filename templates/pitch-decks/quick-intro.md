# Pitch Deck Template: Quick Intro
# Audience: Any — first meeting, cold introduction, exploratory conversation
# Length: 5–6 slides | Target duration: 15–20 minutes
# Use when: First meeting with a new contact, brief introduction slot at an event,
#            executive fly-by, reconnect after a long gap

---

## Slide 1 — Title

**Headline:** Google Cloud — A Conversation with {Company}

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Subtitle | {Month} {Year} |
| Presenter | {AE Name}, {Title}, Google Cloud |
| Customer contact | {Contact Name}, {Title}, {Company} |

**Speaker notes:**
> This is the least important slide in the deck. Get off it quickly. Open with a question, not a pitch: "Before I share anything about Google, can I ask — what's on your plate right now that you're most focused on solving?" Their answer will tell you which of the next slides to emphasize. This deck exists to anchor the conversation, not to be read slide by slide.

**Timing:** 30 seconds — then ask your opening question

---

## Slide 2 — Why We're Here

**Headline:** We Think There's Something Worth Exploring Together

**Content structure:**
- **What we've observed about {Company}:** [2–3 specific observations from research — industry position, growth, tech news, stated strategic priorities from public sources]
- **What we're hearing from {Industry} leaders:** [One industry trend or challenge relevant to them]
- **Why we wanted to have this conversation:** [Honest, specific — e.g., "We're working with several {industry} companies on {specific problem} and the results have been significant enough that we wanted to share them"]

**What we're not here to do:**
- We're not here to sell you anything today
- We're not here to present a 60-slide deck about Google
- We're here to understand your priorities and see if there's a fit

**Speaker notes:**
> This slide sets the tone as consultative, not transactional. The more specific you are about {Company} — their recent announcements, their market position, a challenge their industry is facing — the more credibility you build in the first two minutes. Generic "we help companies like yours" openings lose executives immediately.

**Timing:** 3 minutes (mostly conversation after your opening)

---

## Slide 3 — Three Questions We Hear

**Headline:** The Questions We Get Most Often from {Industry} Leaders

**Question 1:** "{Verbatim-style question — e.g., 'How do we reduce infrastructure costs without taking on migration risk?'}"
- What we've found: [Brief answer — 1–2 sentences]

**Question 2:** "{e.g., 'We're being pushed to do AI — where do we actually start?'}"
- What we've found: [Brief answer — 1–2 sentences]

**Question 3:** "{e.g., 'We have a Microsoft contract expiring next year — is this the moment to evaluate alternatives?'}"
- What we've found: [Brief answer — 1–2 sentences]

**The question for you:** "Which of these, if any, is relevant to where you are right now?"

**Speaker notes:**
> This is the highest-value slide in the deck. The three questions should reflect real conversations — they should feel like "someone told Google what we're discussing in our leadership meetings." When you ask "which of these resonates?" you're doing discovery without making it feel like an interrogation. Listen carefully — their answer defines the rest of your commercial motion with this account.

**Timing:** 5 minutes (mostly their answers)

---

## Slide 4 — What Google Does Differently

**Headline:** Three Things That Are Genuinely Different — Not Marketing

**Differentiator 1: {Most relevant to their expressed interest from slide 3}**
- The claim: [One sentence]
- The proof: [Specific fact, stat, or architecture advantage — not vague]
- Why it matters to {Industry}: [Connect to their world]

**Differentiator 2: {Second most relevant}**
- The claim: [One sentence]
- The proof: [Specific fact, stat, or architecture advantage]
- Why it matters to {Industry}: [Connect to their world]

**Differentiator 3: {Third — often AI/Workspace/sustainability depending on audience}**
- The claim: [One sentence]
- The proof: [Specific fact, stat, or architecture advantage]
- Why it matters to {Industry}: [Connect to their world]

**What we don't do:** [Honest acknowledgment of one area where a competitor is stronger, or one thing Google isn't the best fit for — this builds trust]

**Speaker notes:**
> Do not list 10 differentiators. Pick three that matter to this person based on what you've learned in slides 2–3. The "what we don't do" section is optional but powerful — executives respect honesty and it dramatically increases credibility for everything else you say. Only include it if you can be genuinely honest about a limitation that doesn't kill the deal.

**Timing:** 4 minutes

---

## Slide 5 — One Resonating Story

**Headline:** One Company in {Industry} That Was Facing What You're Facing

**The story:**
- **Company:** {Customer name or "A leading {industry} company in {region}"}
- **Their situation:** [2–3 sentences — what they were dealing with, what they'd tried]
- **What they did with Google:** [Specific, concrete — not "they moved to the cloud"]
- **What happened:** [Quantified outcome — time, money, capability, or risk]
- **What they said:** [Quote if available and approved for use]

**Why this story:** [Connect explicitly to the question from slide 3 that resonated most]

**Speaker notes:**
> Stories stick. Numbers without stories are forgotten. The best reference story is the one that mirrors the customer's situation most closely. If you don't have a named reference, use an industry-type reference: "A mid-size logistics company in Germany" is acceptable. Never fabricate details — if you don't know the exact outcome, say "the results were significant enough that they asked us to feature them in our case study."

**Timing:** 3 minutes

---

## Slide 6 — What Could We Explore?

**Headline:** If Any of This Resonates — Where Should We Go Next?

**Three possible paths:**

**Path A — Technical Exploration**
If the conversation was technical:
"We could schedule a working session with your architects to go deep on {specific area they mentioned}. No commitment, just a 90-minute technical dialogue."

**Path B — Business Case**
If the conversation was commercial:
"We could put together a rough business case using your public data — in two weeks, you'd have a clearer picture of the financial opportunity."

**Path C — Quick Proof**
If there was skepticism to overcome:
"We could set up a small proof of concept — you'd see the platform working with your data in 4–6 weeks, before any commitment."

**Or simply:**
"What would be the most useful next conversation for you?"

**Speaker notes:**
> Never leave without a next step. But give them the choice of next step — it increases the probability they'll say yes and ensures the next meeting is what they actually want. If they say "send me something" without a next meeting, that's usually a polite exit. Push gently: "Happy to send something — what would be the trigger for us to talk again after you've read it?"

**Timing:** 3 minutes

---

## Usage Notes

- This deck should take 15 minutes maximum if you run it straight through. It's designed to be a conversation launcher, not a complete pitch.
- Customize slides 3 and 5 specifically for each meeting — generic content kills the "we've done our homework" effect.
- If the meeting runs long on slide 3 (their answers) — let it. That conversation is more valuable than any subsequent slide.
- For a 10-minute introduction slot: use slides 2, 4, and 6 only.
- For a warm referral introduction: start at slide 3 — skip the "why we're here" setup.
- For Polish-language delivery: translate all narrative content; product names remain in English.
